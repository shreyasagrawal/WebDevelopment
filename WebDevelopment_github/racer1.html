The Racer Game

Using Graphs to Compare Race Cars

Introduction

In this lab, you will be playing an online racing game. Your goal is to determine whether the Bayes car or the Nightingale car is faster. Each car you select can be raced multiple times on a track, and the time to complete each race will be recorded. After you race the cars, the data will be available for you to analyze.

Step 1: Collecting Data

In the game below, you will need to input a Player ID. This will be on the internet, so do not use a name that will identify you. Your instructor will give you a Group ID, which will be identical for every person in the class.

After entering the Player and Group IDs, click on the Tutorial button and complete the tutorial.

Step 2: Exploring Sample Data

Before we analyze the data your class has collected we will first look at a sample dataset, called sample2. In the following visualization app, you first have the Group ID which represents a sample taken from a group of players (i.e. a class). The X Variable can either be Car, Order, or Player ID. The Y Variable can either be Finish Time or Top Speed Reached. Complete the questions on the left to make sure you understand how this app works.

Getting Started:

To use the app below, start with the following settings then answer the questions to the left.

  • Group ID:  sample2
  • X Variable:   Car
  • Y Variable:   Finish Time
  • Check:   Add Boxplot

    • Step 3: Evaluating Your Data

    1. In order to evaluate whether the car speeds are truly different, we should take into account possible confounding variables (variables that the researcher did not include in the study but that might be connected to both the independent variable and the dependent variable). List at least two potential confounding variables that might get in the way of determining which car is faster.

    2. Replace sample2 with the Group ID used by your class.
      3. a. Look carefully through your data. Identify any players that you think should be removed from the dataset.
      b. Do the results from your class agree with the sample2 data?
      c. Do you believe your class tutorial data provides convincing evidence that one car is better than the other?
      d. Based upon the previous question, what evidence did you use to determine that one car was better than another or what additional evidence would be needed before you can determine if one car is better than the other?

    3. Think about different ways we could have conducted this study to improve the quality of our data.
      4. a. Are there better variables to use? For example, what would be the benefits to using Top Speed Reached as the response variable instead of Finish Time?
      b. What are the benefits to randomizing the order of the cars?
      c. How many races would need to be completed to convince you that one car is better than the other?
